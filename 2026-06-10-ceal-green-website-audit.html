Executive Summary

The CEAL Green website demonstrates ambition and technical breadth, but it is operating in direct conflict with its own approved brand identity on multiple critical dimensions. The visual system deployed does not match the approved colour palette, the logo name displayed does not match the approved brand name, and the predominant tone of voice is generic green-consultancy language that the brand guidelines explicitly prohibit. These are not polish issues — they represent a fundamental disconnect between the brand that has been built in strategy and the brand that is being presented to the market.


The site's architecture also contains a navigation element ("Green Energy Projects") that resolves to an e-commerce shop, actively undermining positioning as an execution engine. Until the critical findings are resolved, the site cannot effectively do the job asked of it: establish CEAL Green as a credible, Caribbean-specific, practitioner-led execution engine and convert qualified prospects to consultation.

Critical Findings
9 findings — Must be resolved before the site can do its job
#1
Critical ✗ Change
Global — Visual Identity / Colour System

The website's primary accent colour is a neon/lime green (approximately #00FF7F or similar) deployed as the dominant interactive and highlight colour across all six pages. The approved brand colour system specifies Mangrove (#1E4D3A) as primary, Mid Canopy (#8DCFAA) as secondary green, and Aged Teak (#B87A4E) as accent. The neon green in use does not appear in the approved palette. It contradicts the brand's character — the approved palette is grounded, warm, and Caribbean-referencing; the deployed neon green reads as generic tech-startup green.

Recommendation

Route to Pixel/Iris to audit all colour usage site-wide and replace deployed neon green with the approved palette. Mangrove (#1E4D3A) should carry primary UI weight. Mid Canopy (#8DCFAA) should carry accent and interactive weight. Aged Teak (#B87A4E) for secondary accent. The background dark tone may be revisited against Oatmeal (#F7F4EE) as primary light surface — a near-black background is not in the approved palette either. This is a full-site implementation issue requiring developer involvement.

#2
Critical ✗ Change
Global — Brand Name / Header Identity

The site header displays "CEAL Green Energy Limited" as the wordmark/company name, and the Case Studies page header displays the sub-line "Caribbean Renewable Energy Solutions." Neither matches the approved brand name, which is "CEAL Green." "CEAL Green Energy Limited" is the legal entity name, not the public brand name. Displaying the legal name as the primary brand identifier weakens brand recognition and contradicts the approved wordmark specification.

Recommendation

Replace all public-facing brand name instances with "CEAL Green." The legal name "CEAL Green Energy Limited" belongs in the footer copyright line and legal documents only. The sub-line "Caribbean Renewable Energy Solutions" should be removed — it is a descriptor, not a brand element, and it dilutes the wordmark. Route to developer.

#2b
Critical ✗ Change
Global — Logo / Brand Identity

The logo currently deployed does not meet brand specification. The circular badge mark reads as an eco-certification seal or NGO badge — not as a B2B execution engine. It fails legibility tests at nav scale (32–40px) and favicon scale (16–32px). The arc tagline "Engineering a Resilient Future" embedded in the mark is a brand voice violation: it makes a future promise, which the brand guidelines explicitly prohibit.

Recommendation

Replace the current deployed logo with the revised version currently in development. Do not implement interim fixes to the existing mark.

#3
Critical ✗ Change
Home — Hero Section / Headline

The hero headline reads: "Practical pathways to lower energy costs and build resilient, sustainable operations." This fails on three brand standards simultaneously: (1) it is future-implicit and explanatory rather than present-tense and declarative — "pathways to lower costs" is a promise, not a statement of what is done; (2) it is written from the client's problem frame, not from CEAL Green's identity; (3) "sustainable operations" is generic and on the brand's avoid list. The hero is the single highest-value copy position on the site. A visitor who reads only this line has no sense of who CEAL Green is — only what problem they might solve.

Recommendation

Replace with a present-tense, identity-led headline that names who CEAL Green is and states what it does, with Caribbean specificity. The tagline "Bridging the Caribbean's Green Gap" has more energy than the headline but is also passive — evaluate together.

#4
Critical ✗ Change
Home — Vision/Mission Statements

The published Vision reads: "To be the Caribbean's leading catalyst for the green energy transition, empowering resilient communities, competitive businesses, and climate-smart infrastructure." This contains two prohibited terms in a single sentence: "catalyst" (advocacy register) and "green energy transition" (generic aspirational language). The Mission reads: "To design, enable and deliver integrated renewable energy and sustainability solutions that reduce carbon emissions..." — "sustainability solutions" is generic and the Mission/Vision format contradicts the brand's identity-declaration standard ("We state what we do, not what we intend").

Recommendation

Replace Vision/Mission format entirely with identity-declaration statements grounded in the approved brand foundation. The guiding narrative's Core Proposition and "The Promise" section are the approved equivalents.

#5
Critical ✗ Change
Green Energy Projects — Page / Navigation Mismatch

The navigation item "Green Energy Projects" resolves to a page titled "Green Energy Products" that functions as an e-commerce shop, displaying a minimum payment amount of TTD$7.00 and product descriptions for solar panels, batteries, wind turbines, EV chargers, and smart infrastructure. This is a content-architecture failure: (a) it actively undermines positioning as an execution engine by presenting an e-commerce shop front in the primary navigation; (b) it misleads visitors who navigate there expecting a case study or project portfolio experience; (c) the TTD$7 minimum payment amount reads as a development artifact or placeholder and damages credibility.

Recommendation

This requires an IA and content decision before any design or copy work. Three options: (1) Remove "Green Energy Projects" from primary navigation entirely until a genuine project portfolio page is built; (2) Rename the nav item to "Green Energy Products" to accurately reflect the page; (3) Build a genuine projects/portfolio page and route the shop to a separate, lower-priority path. Option 1 is the brand-safe choice given current positioning. Route to Ryan Roper for decision, then developer for implementation.

#6
Critical ✗ Change
Home — Geographic Scope / Brand Boundary

The "Why CEAL Green" section states: "We accelerate sustainability projects across the Caribbean and Latin America — from concept to execution and operations." Every approved brand document is explicitly Caribbean-only. "Latin America" appears nowhere in the approved brand foundation. This claim extends the geographic scope without strategic or evidential foundation, undermines the brand's Caribbean-specific positioning which is explicitly named as a differentiator, and contradicts the brand principle: "Not generic — no claims that could apply to any green company, anywhere."

Recommendation

Remove "and Latin America" from this statement and from any other instance on the site. If a Latin American expansion is planned, that is a future scope announcement that requires strategic framing — not a present-tense claim inserted into a brand positioning statement.

The names Marcus Williams, Sophia Rodriguez, and David Chen do not read as Caribbean names. If they are placeholder/generated content, remove them before any further marketing activity. Fabricated testimonials are a legal and brand integrity risk that must be resolved before this audit's other findings are acted on.

#8
Critical ✗ Change
Home — Partner Logos Section

The home page displays partner organisation logos for: Caribbean Development Bank, CARICOM Energy, IDB Invest, UN Environment, GIZ Caribbean, and World Bank — presented without qualification under implicit partnership framing. Displaying logos of the Caribbean Development Bank, World Bank, and UN Environment as implied partners without confirmed contractual or project relationships is a credibility and legal risk. If these are aspirational affiliations, displaying their logos is misrepresentation.

For confirmed members/affiliates: add the membership qualifier. For unconfirmed: remove logos until the relationship is established and legally cleared. This is a brand integrity and legal issue, not a copy issue.

Major Findings
19 findings — Significant brand or conversion impact; resolve in next sprint
#9
Major ✗ Change
Global Navigation — Login/Register

The primary navigation includes "Login" and "Register" links (rendered as an "Account" dropdown) alongside the brand navigation. A visitor to a B2B professional services and infrastructure company has no context for why they would log in or register. The presence of an account system without explanation suggests either a platform product not yet announced or an e-commerce/subscription layer not integrated into the brand narrative. For a site trying to establish credibility as a practitioner-led execution engine, unexplained account functionality reads as unfinished.

Recommendation

Either (a) contextualise the account system — explain what registered users access (e.g., the Greenularity carbon monitoring platform, project dashboards) — or (b) move Login/Register to the footer or a secondary "Platform" section, removing it from primary navigation. If the platform is not yet publicly launched, remove from navigation entirely. Route decision to Ryan Roper; implementation to developer.

#10
Major ✗ Change
Global — Primary CTA / Brand Register

The primary CTA throughout the site is "Book Now" in the header navigation. Secondary CTAs include "Book A Call," "Book A Consultation," and "Book my Consultation." The brand voice specifies "Invitational — Pulls towards something, never pressures or recruits." "Book Now" is a pressure/urgency register more appropriate for e-commerce than for a professional services execution engine. The word "Now" imports scarcity framing that contradicts the brand's constructive register. The inconsistency among CTAs across the site also signals a lack of deliberate CTA strategy.

Recommendation

Standardise the primary CTA to an invitational formulation. "Book A Consultation" is the most appropriate — specific, professional, non-pressuring. The header button should be consistent with in-page CTAs. Remove "Now" from the navigation CTA.

#11
Major ✗ Change
Home — "How It Works" Process Section

Step 3 of the "How It Works" section is labelled "Book A Call" — the same label as Step 1. The process reads: (1) Book A Call → (2) We Assess Your Needs → (3) Book A Call. Step 3 is a duplicate of Step 1 and appears to be a content error (likely intended to be "We Build" or "We Deliver" or similar). This is a credibility-damaging error on a section designed to demonstrate structured process capability.

Recommendation

Correct Step 3 label to accurately reflect what it describes — the execution/delivery phase. The Step 3 description already refers to "complete roadmap and execution," confirming the label is wrong.

#12
Major ✗ Change
Case Studies — "View Full Case Study" Links

All four case study "View Full Case Study" buttons link to "#" — placeholder anchors with no destination. For a site whose primary conversion goal is to establish credibility and convert to consultation, broken case study links actively undermine the single most important trust-building content type available. A visitor who clicks any of these four buttons will have their exploration path terminated.

Recommendation

Either (a) build and publish individual case study detail pages — the highest-priority content development investment available, as case studies are the primary proof of execution capability — or (b) replace the buttons with a contact/inquiry path ("Discuss a similar project → Book A Consultation") until detail pages are built. Do not leave dead links active.

Narrative Coherence Assessment
3 structural gaps in the full-site narrative arc
Full-site arc: The intended narrative arc is: Caribbean problem → CEAL Green capability → proof of execution → call to act. The site has the structural elements for this arc but deploys them in a fragmented and inconsistent way. The Home page attempts all five moves simultaneously, creating density without flow. The Our Solutions page re-explains the process already covered on the Home page rather than deepening trust. The Case Studies page carries the strongest trust content but dead-links destroy its function. The About Us page introduces leadership credibility late and buries it under generic differentiators. The Green Energy Projects page breaks the arc entirely by resolving to an e-commerce shop.
NC-01
Execution proof arrives too late
The Case Studies page is the primary proof of execution, but it is the third item in the navigation, reached only by deliberate intent. The home page has three "Featured Solutions" cards that link to /services but no direct path to case evidence. A qualified prospect evaluating whether to book a consultation needs to encounter real project proof earlier in the journey — ideally within the Home page flow before the consultation CTA.
NC-02
Identity declaration gap: site opens on problem framing
The hero opens on the visitor's problem ("lower energy costs"), not on CEAL Green's identity. The approved brand foundation leads with identity ("CEAL Green is a Caribbean execution engine for green solutions"). This sequence inversion means the brand never fully declares itself before beginning to explain its service. Every benchmark in the competitive set leads with identity or outcome — not with the client's problem.
NC-03
Green Energy Projects page breaks the trust architecture
A visitor who navigates to "Green Energy Projects" encounters an e-commerce shop page with a TTD$7 minimum payment. This is a complete trust break. At the point where brand and execution credibility have been building, an unexpected retail endpoint causes visitors to question what kind of company they are dealing with. This is the single most damaging structural issue on the site from a conversion architecture perspective.

Key Patterns — What Best-in-Class Consistently Does
Pattern 1
Lead with specific outcomes, not process descriptions. Sunrock and Enphase lead their hero sections with named quantified outcomes ("X MW installed," "X% bill reduction") rather than descriptions of what they do. CEAL Green's hero describes a journey ("pathways to lower energy costs") rather than declaring a result. This is a missed opportunity on the highest-value real estate on the site.
Pattern 2
Credibility proof is above the fold or one click away. DNV surfaces certifications, named project references, and named client logos with relationship qualifiers within the first scroll of the homepage. Actis surfaces named portfolio companies immediately. CEAL Green buries its case study proof behind a navigation click and then dead-links.
Pattern 3
Partner/client logo sections carry relationship qualifiers. Actis and DNV present client/partner logos with explicit relationship context ("advisory mandate for," "certification partner on," "investor in"). Presenting logos without qualifiers — as CEAL Green does with Caribbean Development Bank and World Bank — is a pattern that sophisticated audiences (government, international capital) immediately notice and discount.
Pattern 4
Distinct visual identity from generic green-tech. Both Sunrock and DNV use visual systems that are clearly branded and distinct from the neon-green-on-dark palette that has become generic in the tech-adjacent sustainability space. DNV's deep blue carries authority. Sunrock uses warm earth tones with Caribbean sunlight references. CEAL Green's approved palette (Mangrove, Oatmeal, Aged Teak) would achieve exactly this distinction — but the deployed neon green places it in the generic tech-startup category.
Pattern 5
Mobile-first CTA persistence. Enphase and Sunrock maintain a persistent mobile CTA that scrolls with the page. CEAL Green's "Book Now" in the header is not described as persistent on mobile — if it disappears on scroll, conversion events are lost.
Competitive Advantage
CEAL Green's Differentiator the Benchmark Set Does Not Replicate
CEAL Green has one advantage the benchmark set does not replicate: the ROI Calculator. Sunrock, DNV, and Actis do not offer an immediate, personalised savings estimate on-site. This interactive conversion tool, if fixed (see Finding #18 re residential audience and Finding #31 re sample data), is a genuine differentiator that should be emphasised more prominently in the site's narrative flow.

The leadership team depth (five named directors with Caribbean credentials) is also stronger than Sunrock's public-facing team presentation, and comparable to DNV's advisory board format. This asset is underserved by the generic differentiator copy (Finding #36).
